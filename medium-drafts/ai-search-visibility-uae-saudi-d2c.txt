Gulf Brands Have Great Products. ChatGPT Doesn't Know They Exist.

The UAE and Saudi Arabia are among the fastest-growing digital commerce markets in the world. But when buyers there ask AI for recommendations, international brands answer the question. Here is why, and what closes the gap.

- - -

I was running buying queries for a Gulf-based beauty brand. Queries in English first: "best moisturiser for humid climate." Then in Arabic. On ChatGPT, on Perplexity, on Gemini.

The brand didn't appear in a single response.

The competitor that appeared consistently was a European brand with thinner local relevance, longer shipping times, and no Arabic content at all. It showed up because it had something the Gulf brand didn't: structured content that AI systems could parse, cite, and deliver as an answer. The Gulf brand had a beautiful website, strong Instagram presence, and a loyal customer base. But it had no Arabic-language product pages, no reviews on Noon or Amazon.ae, and no content specifically addressing what Gulf buyers ask about when they search for skincare.

That gap, not product quality, is why AI keeps recommending the wrong brand in the Gulf.

The Gulf AI Market: Massive Adoption, Zero Brand Readiness

The numbers are striking. Saudi Arabia's internet penetration reached 99% and smartphone penetration exceeded 96%, according to CITC 2025. UAE's e-commerce penetration already exceeds 75%. Saudi Arabia's e-commerce market is estimated at $31.3 billion in 2026, growing rapidly under the Vision 2030 digital transformation agenda. Gulf consumers are among the most digitally sophisticated buyers anywhere.

And they are using AI. ChatGPT, Gemini, and Perplexity all see significant Gulf usage. Arabic is a supported query language on all three platforms. The UAE National Strategy for Artificial Intelligence has positioned the country as a global AI hub. Saudi Arabia's SDAIA is driving AI adoption across government and private sector alike.

But here is the gap I keep seeing when I audit Gulf brands: while Gulf consumers are actively using AI for product discovery, Gulf brands have not adapted their content for AI extraction. The result is a widening situation where international competitors capture AI recommendations that should belong to local brands with better regional relevance, better logistics, and more cultural fit.

Gulf-Specific Trust Signals AI Platforms Look For

AI platforms evaluate brands across buying decision factors: Trust, Use Case Fit, Pricing, Ease of Use, and Quality. In the Gulf market, these factors show up differently than in the US or Europe.

For Trust, the primary signals are verified purchase reviews on Noon and Amazon.ae, not global Amazon.com. Regional marketplace reviews carry more weight for Gulf-specific queries. A brand with 500 Amazon.com reviews and zero Noon presence is less credible to AI evaluating a Gulf buyer query.

Regional editorial coverage matters too. Mentions in Arabian Business, Gulf News, Arab News, and Khaleej Times signal regional authority. These publications are weighted heavily by AI systems for Gulf market queries in a way that international publications simply aren't.

Government certifications, ESMA for UAE and SFDA for Saudi Arabia, serve as institutional trust markers. Halal certification is a non-negotiable baseline for food, beauty, and personal care. These are not just legal requirements. They are trust signals that AI systems actively look for when deciding which brand to recommend.

The Bilingual Content Gap: Why English Alone Doesn't Work

Here is the pattern I see most often when auditing AI visibility for Gulf brands: excellent English content, strong Instagram presence, good Google rankings. And complete invisibility in Arabic-language AI queries.

This matters because a significant portion of Gulf consumers interact with AI tools in Arabic. When a consumer in Riyadh types an Arabic query asking for the best moisturiser for hot, dry climates, the AI draws from Arabic-language sources. If your brand has no Arabic content, you do not exist in that conversation.

This is not about translating your English website. Gulf consumers ask product questions differently than US or European buyers. They ask about climate suitability, humidity considerations, Halal compliance, and regional shipping. "هل هذا المنتج مناسب للطقس الحار؟" (Is this product suitable for hot weather?) requires Arabic-language content that addresses Gulf-specific conditions, not a translated English product page.

The brands investing in Arabic AI-ready content now are establishing citation dominance before competitors recognise the opportunity. That first-mover advantage is the same one early GEO adopters captured in English-language markets, and it compounds.

Five Actions for Gulf D2C AI Visibility

These are the five actions I consistently see produce measurable citation movement for Gulf brands. Each one addresses a specific gap that causes AI to skip local brands in favour of international competitors.

1

**Build Arabic-language use-case content for your top 10 buyer queries**
Identify the 10 most common Arabic-language queries buyers use when searching for products in your category. Create dedicated Arabic content pages that answer each query with specific, structured, AI-extractable information. Include pricing in AED and SAR, regional availability, and Gulf-specific use cases. This single action addresses the largest visibility gap for most Gulf D2C brands.

2

**Establish verified review presence on Noon and Amazon.ae**
Global Amazon reviews do not transfer to Gulf AI queries. AI platforms evaluating Gulf market recommendations prioritise reviews from regional platforms: Noon, Amazon.ae, and regional platforms like Faces and Sephora Middle East. A structured post-purchase review collection programme targeting these platforms builds the regional trust signal that AI systems need to cite your brand for Gulf-specific queries.

3

**Create Gulf-specific comparison and recommendation content**
When a buyer asks ChatGPT for the best product in your category available in Dubai, the AI looks for content that explicitly positions your brand within the Gulf market context. Create comparison pages that address Gulf-specific constraints: availability in UAE and KSA, shipping timelines within the GCC, climate suitability, regulatory compliance, and regional price competitiveness.

4

**Pursue regional editorial coverage in Gulf business and lifestyle publications**
A mention in Arabian Business, Gulf News, or a category-specific Gulf publication creates the editorial trust signal that AI platforms weight heavily for regional queries. A targeted PR strategy focused on 5-10 relevant Gulf publications builds regional authority that translates directly into AI citation probability.

5

**Add regulatory compliance and certification signals to your content**
ESMA certification, SFDA approval, and Halal certification are trust signals that AI platforms actively look for when evaluating Gulf market recommendations. These certifications should be explicitly stated in structured format on your product pages, not buried in footer text. AI systems extract and weight these signals when deciding which brand to recommend for Gulf-specific queries.
